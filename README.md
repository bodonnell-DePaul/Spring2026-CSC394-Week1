## 📖 Course Overview

This is a project-centered software engineering course built around the design, implementation, deployment, and presentation of a substantial team software product. Across prior versions of this course, the consistent themes have been:

- A substantial team project as the center of the class.
- Recurring milestone demos.
- Strong individual accountability inside group work.
- Emphasis on documentation, planning, and professional communication.
- Regular in-class working time with instructor feedback.
- A strong expectation that project deliverables must function when demonstrated.

This version of CSC 394 keeps those common foundations while explicitly incorporating modern AI-supported software development practices. GitHub Copilot is encouraged as a core tool for brainstorming, coding, testing, refactoring, documentation, and debugging. Students are expected to use AI responsibly, verify all outputs, and remain accountable for every technical and written submission.

## 🎯 Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

1. Elicit and refine functional and non-functional requirements for a software product.
2. Work effectively in a project team with clearly defined responsibilities and handoffs.
3. Design and implement APIs, data models, front-end integrations, and cloud-hosted services.
4. Use version control, issue tracking, CI/CD pipelines, and deployment tooling in a professional workflow.
5. Use AI tools, including GitHub Copilot, to accelerate software development while maintaining quality, attribution, and accountability.
6. Produce architecture diagrams, technical documentation, demo materials, and project presentations.
7. Evaluate project risks, test effectively, and deliver working software on milestone deadlines.

## 🏗️ Course Structure

Each class meeting follows the same overall pattern:

- 🎓 **Short lecture:** A focused topic on software engineering practice, team process, architecture, delivery, or AI-assisted development.
- 🔍 **Project checkpoint:** Instructor-led review of progress, blockers, and next milestones.
- 🛠️ **Structured studio time:** Teams work in class on planning, implementation, testing, documentation, and demos.

Because the course is studio-based, consistent attendance and active participation are essential. Students should expect to make measurable progress during every class session.

## 💻 Project-Based Format

Students will work in teams to build a substantial software project over the quarter. The project should include, at minimum:

- A clear problem statement and target users.
- Documented functional and non-functional requirements.
- An API and back-end service layer.
- Data storage and deployment infrastructure.
- A usable front end or client experience.
- Test coverage appropriate to the scope of the project.
- Deployment and operational documentation.
- A polished final demonstration.

There will be no traditional midterm or final exam. Evaluation is based on project milestones, demos, documentation, participation, and individual contribution.

## 👥 Team Roles

Each team member must hold a clearly defined role. Larger teams may assign one role per person; smaller teams may combine roles with instructor approval. Every responsibility must have a named owner.

Suggested roles include:

- **Project Manager:** coordinates deadlines, agendas, and team accountability.
- **Planning Manager / Scrum Lead:** maintains sprint plans, issue tracking, and milestone readiness.
- **Requirements Manager / Product Lead:** owns requirements, user stories, and scope decisions.
- **Chief Designer / Architect:** leads architecture, system boundaries, interfaces, and technical direction.
- **Implementation Lead:** coordinates coding standards, integration, and delivery of core features.
- **Testing Manager / QA Lead:** owns test planning, quality gates, regression checks, and demo readiness.
- **DevOps / Cloud Lead:** owns environments, CI/CD, deployment, infrastructure, and monitoring.
- **Documentation Manager:** owns technical documentation, setup instructions, and project manual quality.
- **Collaboration Tools Manager:** maintains repository hygiene, branching workflow, task board usage, and shared artifacts.
- **Presentation Manager:** prepares demo flow, presentation materials, and speaking coordination.

All students remain responsible for understanding the full system, not only their assigned area.

Minimum role expectations by team size:

- **Teams of 3-4:** At minimum, assign a Product/Requirements owner, a Technical/Implementation lead, and a Testing/Delivery owner.
- **Teams of 5 or more:** assign distinct owners for planning, requirements, architecture, implementation, testing, deployment, documentation, and presentation.
- Role ownership will be evaluated as part of the individual contribution grade.

## 🤖 Use of AI and GitHub Copilot

AI use is encouraged in this course. GitHub Copilot is the recommended default tool for day-to-day development work.

Appropriate uses include:

- Generating starter code, tests, and documentation.
- Exploring alternative implementations.
- Debugging and explaining code.
- Refactoring for readability and maintainability.
- Creating draft API contracts, diagrams, and user stories.
- Accelerating repetitive engineering tasks.

Students are expected to follow these rules:

1. **You are accountable for all submitted work.** AI assistance does not transfer responsibility.
2. **Verify everything.** Test generated code, validate assumptions, and confirm security and correctness.
3. **Disclose meaningful AI use.** Every major milestone submission must include a 1-3 sentence note describing how AI tools were used, for example, in coding, testing, documentation, debugging, or design.
4. **Do not submit fabricated citations, invented results, or unverifiable claims.**
5. **Do not use AI to avoid understanding the system.** You must be able to explain and defend your design and implementation decisions.
6. **Protect confidential information.** Do not paste secrets, credentials, private student data, or proprietary code into external systems unless explicitly approved.

AI use that obscures authorship, bypasses understanding, or misrepresents effort will be treated as an academic integrity issue. Submitting AI-generated text, code, or analysis as if it were entirely your own work without disclosure is not acceptable.

## 🛠️ Tools and Expected Workflow

Teams are expected to use a modern collaborative workflow. At minimum, each team should maintain:

- A shared Git repository.
- A task board or issue tracker.
- A documented branching and review process.
- A deployable application environment.
- A central project manual or technical handbook.

Recommended tooling includes GitHub, GitHub Projects or an equivalent tracker, GitHub Actions or another CI/CD system, and a cloud deployment platform such as AWS, Azure, or another approved hosting environment.

The default technical baseline for the course is:

- A web API.
- A persistent data store.
- A client application or front end.
- Automated tests.
- A CI/CD pipeline.
- A deployed environment.

Teams may choose their own stack, but they must be able to justify it and support it operationally.

## 📊 Assessment and Grading

The grading structure below reflects the most common patterns across the prior syllabi: substantial weight on the shared project, a meaningful individual component, and a distinct participation and professionalism component.

| Component | Weight |
| --- | ---: |
| Team project milestones and demos | 60% |
| Individual contribution, peer assessment, and reflection | 20% |
| Attendance, participation, professionalism, and weekly progress updates | 20% |

The **team project milestones and demos (60%)** are divided as follows:

| Milestone | Weight |
| --- | ---: |
| Project pitch and requirements package | 10% |
| Architecture, API, and delivery plan | 10% |
| Concept demo | 10% |
| Midterm check-in demo | 10% |
| Preview demo | 5% |
| Final demo, presentation, and project documentation | 15% |

The **individual contribution component (20%)** is divided as follows:

| Individual Component | Weight |
| --- | ---: |
| Peer and team evaluation | 8% |
| Role ownership and contribution evidence | 7% |
| Individual reflection or contribution memo | 5% |

Individual grades may be adjusted upward or downward from the shared team project grade when contribution evidence, peer feedback, missed responsibilities, or professionalism issues justify it.

The **attendance, participation, professionalism, and weekly progress component (20%)** includes:

- Attendance in class.
- Engagement during team working sessions.
- Preparation for milestone reviews.
- Constructive participation in discussion and critique.
- Professional communication with teammates and the instructor.

Detailed rubrics for each major milestone will be shared no later than one week before the due date.

## ✅ Attendance and Class Expectations

Attendance is mandatory because the course depends on in-class collaboration, milestone feedback, and working software reviews.

Students are expected to:

- Arrive on time and remain engaged for the full class period.
- Attend scheduled team meetings.
- Make visible weekly progress.
- Come prepared to discuss blockers and next steps.
- Contribute professionally to planning, coding, testing, and documentation.
- Communicate early when issues affect participation or deadlines.

Unexcused absences will lower the participation and individual contribution portions of the course grade. Repeated missed classes or missed team meetings will also be reflected in peer evaluations and milestone readiness.

Attendance formula:

- Each unexcused class absence reduces the attendance and participation component by 10 percentage points.
- Missing a required team meeting or failing to participate in an assigned in-class team work session may be treated the same way.
- Documented emergencies should be communicated as early as possible.

## 🎤 Demo Expectations

Demos are a central part of the course. Across prior versions of CSC 394, the strongest shared expectation was simple: the project must function on demo day.

For every demo:

1. The system must run in a way that the team can explain and demonstrate.
2. The demo must clearly connect requirements, implementation, and current progress.
3. Teams must be prepared to discuss architecture, testing status, deployment status, and known risks.
4. Every team member should be able to speak to the overall system, not only their assigned role.
5. Teams should bring a fallback plan, but a demo that does not run at all cannot earn higher than 50% of that milestone's grade and receives a zero for the live-functionality portion of the rubric.

Required demos:

- **Concept Demo:** early proof of direction, technical feasibility, and team understanding.
- **Midterm Check-In Demo:** working vertical slice showing real progress across the stack.
- **Preview Demo:** rehearsal-quality demonstration with major risk areas addressed.
- **Final Demo and Presentation:** polished end-to-end demonstration of the project, supported by documentation.

All students are expected to follow DePaul's Academic Integrity Policy. Cheating, plagiarism, unauthorized assistance, fabrication, falsification, and misrepresentation of work are violations of university policy and may result in course penalties and referral through university procedures.

Course materials, assignment solutions, project artifacts, and instructor-generated content may not be posted to public websites or content-sharing services unless explicitly authorized. Sharing or using unauthorized posted material may be treated as an academic integrity violation.

DePaul is a community built on respect, professionalism, and open dialogue. Students are expected to engage in constructive disagreement without bullying, intimidation, domination of discussion, or dismissive conduct. Team-based work requires reliability, respect for others' time, and accountable follow-through.

Students are responsible for understanding university deadlines for registration, withdrawal, and grading policies. Incomplete grades are granted only in exceptional circumstances consistent with university policy.

Students are welcome to share the name and pronouns they would like used in class. The course will respect students' identities and support the use of preferred name information available through university systems.

Students seeking accommodations should work with the Center for Students with Disabilities (CSD) as early as possible. Students who have arranged accommodations through CSD are encouraged to communicate with the instructor early in the quarter so those accommodations can be implemented smoothly.

### 💚 Student Support and Well-Being
